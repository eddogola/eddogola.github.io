Samaki AI

MIT Sloan Africa Innovation Conference Hackathon, 2025

View Project →

This was my first hackathon. I built Samaki AI with three guys from Ghana — two at MIT, one at Harvard — over a frantic weekend for the MIT Sloan Africa Innovation Conference. The idea: an autonomous fish feeder that watches the fish and decides when and how much to feed, using computer vision instead of a timer.

Samaki is Swahili for "fish." The system uses a camera to watch underwater, runs a vision pipeline to pick up on whether the fish are hungry or satisfied, and drives a stepper-motor auger to dispense the right amount of feed. Overfeeding is a real problem in aquaculture — uneaten feed decays and fouls the water — so getting this right actually matters.

What I worked on

I ended up spending most of my time on hardware and mechanical design, which honestly was the most fun part for me. I'd only picked up CAD about two weeks before the hackathon — just enough to be dangerous in Onshape — so designing the auger assembly and enclosure was equal parts exciting and humbling. I loved thinking through how the software side would physically manifest: where does the stepper mount, how does the auger tube sit, how do you keep electronics dry near water.

The intersection of hardware and software is genuinely interesting to me, and this project was a good first taste of that. It also made me want to go deeper — I left the hackathon curious about electronics in a way I hadn't been before, and it's pushed me to want to properly learn PCB design.

The pitch

We stumbled a bit during the presentation — first hackathon nerves, coordination across four people who'd just met, the usual. Something to work on for next time. The building part felt stronger than the pitching part, which I suppose is a decent problem to have.

How it works

Vision pipeline

YOLOv8/v12 handles detection and tracks individual fish in real time using ByteTrack. A DINOv2-based classifier runs downstream to read behavioral signals — aggregation patterns, surface agitation, opercular movement — as proxies for hunger or satiation. Each frame produces 20+ features logged to tracks.json, which feeds a digital twin and historical growth analysis.

  • Detection: YOLOv8 / YOLOv12 + ByteTrack
  • Classification: DINOv2 fine-tuned for aquatic behavior
  • Runtime: Python 3.10+, PyTorch, OpenCV

Hardware

The mechanical side is built around a 12V helical auger. A Sony IMX327 keeps the pipeline working in low light; a ToF LiDAR monitors how full the hopper is. Power supports both USB-PD and a solar-ready 12V rail for off-grid use.

Firmware

The STM32 translates an inferred feed-mass value into stepper steps and pulls the enable line low only during an active dispense cycle to keep heat down. 

void dispenseFeed(float grams) {
    int steps = calculateSteps(grams);
    digitalWrite(STEPPER_ENABLE, LOW);
    for (int i = 0; i < steps; i++) {
        digitalWrite(STEP_PIN, HIGH);
        delayMicroseconds(500);
        digitalWrite(STEP_PIN, LOW);
        delayMicroseconds(500);
    }
    digitalWrite(STEPPER_ENABLE, HIGH);
}
